\section*{Abstract}

South Africa's NATCOR rail corridor is the country's principal freight
artery, connecting the inland economic heartland to the ports of
Durban and Richards Bay. In April 2022, catastrophic flooding across
KwaZulu-Natal disrupted rail infrastructure, operations, and supply
chains at a scale not seen in decades. Quantifying the causal impact of
this shock on freight volumes is essential for evidence-based
infrastructure planning and disaster-resilience policy, yet most
existing analyses rely on associative forecasting methods that cannot
isolate the effect of a single intervention from confounding trends.

This thesis applies a Synthetic Control Method (SCM) framework to
estimate the causal effect of the KZN floods on NATCOR monthly rail
freight volume (million tonnes, 2015--2024). Five estimators are
compared: the standard constrained SCM, the Augmented SCM (ASCM) with
ridge bias-correction, Elastic Net SCM (EN-SCM), Matrix Completion SCM
(MC-SCM) via singular value thresholding, and Synthetic
Difference-in-Differences (SDID). The treated unit is NATCOR; the donor
pool consists of five South African freight corridors --- CAPE,
IRON\_ORE, NE\_CORRIDOR, NORTHCOR, and SOUTHCOR.

A key empirical finding of the study is methodological as much as
substantive: when only a single donor corridor is available, the five
estimators produce wildly inconsistent results, ranging from
$-11.1$\,MT to $+3.7$\,MT cumulative effect. Once an adequate
five-corridor donor pool is constructed, all five methods converge
tightly, estimating a cumulative loss of approximately
$\mathbf{-5.1}$\,\textbf{to}\,$\mathbf{-5.2}$\,\textbf{MT} over the
33 post-flood months (average $-0.155$ to $-0.159$\,MT/month). Every
estimator passes in-time placebo tests and leave-one-out donor
stability checks, providing robust causal evidence that the 2022 KZN
floods caused a sustained, economically significant reduction in
NATCOR freight volume that persisted well beyond the immediate
disruption period.
